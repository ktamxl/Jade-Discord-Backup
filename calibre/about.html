The Art of the Movement

Why this collection exists.

Why Document Watchmaking?

Mechanical watchmaking is one of the few crafts where the product is simultaneously a precision instrument, a work of art, and a philosophical object. A watch movement contains dozens of interdependent mechanisms — each one requiring thousands of hours of human skill to produce — all working in concert to measure something as abstract as the passage of time.

This collection exists to document that. Not as a catalogue of objects, but as a record of the ideas, the craftsmanship, and the centuries of accumulated knowledge that each piece contains. When you look through the caseback of a Lange movement and see the hand-applied Glashutte stripes catching the light, you are looking at the output of a tradition that stretches back to Ferdinand Adolph Lange in 1845.

When you read the time from a Patek Philippe dial, you are reading it from a surface that took an entire day to prepare. When you feel the column wheel click under your finger on a chronograph pusher, you are touching a mechanism that was refined over a century of continuous development.

"A watch is a poem in steel. Each gear is a word, each jewel a syllable, and the balance wheel — the heart — keeps the rhythm."

— Watchmakers maxim

The Watchmakers in This Collection

A. Lange and Sohne — Founded 1845 in Glashutte, Saxony. Suppressed by the Soviet government after WWII, dormant for 45 years, resurrected in 1994 by Walter Lange and Gunter Blumlein. Today produces fewer than 6,000 watches per year. Its movements are widely considered the finest in the world.

Patek Philippe — Founded 1839 in Geneva. Has never been sold or acquired. Produces fewer than 60,000 watches per year. Holds the record for the most expensive watch ever sold at auction: the Henry Graves Supercomplication at $24 million USD in 2014.

The Craft in Detail

Each movement in this collection is hand-finished to a standard that is invisible to the naked eye in normal wear. Beveled edges are polished to a mirror finish using a chamfering tool. Surfaces are decorated with Cotes de Geneve (parallel stripes) and Glashutte stripes — each applied by hand with a rotating peg tool. These finishes do not improve the watch function. They prove that human hands made this.

The balance wheel — oscillating at 21,600 or 28,800 times per hour — is the heart of the watch. Its frequency determines the timekeeping precision: higher frequency means smoother sweep of the second hand and better resistance to shocks, at the cost of slightly higher power consumption.
