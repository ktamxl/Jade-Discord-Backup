The Movement

L951.5 — A Masterclass in Mechanical Finishing

The L951.5 calibre is, by any objective measure, one of the finest hand-wound chronograph movements in existence. It comprises 346 individual components, each hand-finished to a standard set not by industrial efficiency, but by the judgment of a single craftsman.

The three-quarter plate — crafted from German silver, not brass — is decorated with Glashutte stripe finishing: parallel bevels polished by hand across the entire surface. The process removes imperfections at the microscopic level. It takes an entire day to finish one movement plate this way.

The balance cock — engraved with the traditional Glashutte florette — is unique to each watchmaker. No two are engraved identically. It is, in the truest sense, a signature.

The Maison

Glashutte — A Village That Rewrote Time

In the ore-rich hills of Saxony, the town of Glashutte gave its name to an entire tradition of watchmaking. By the mid-19th century, it had become Germanys answer to Geneva — a place where precision mechanics were elevated to fine art.

Ferdinand Adolph Lange founded the original manufacture in 1845, training a generation of watchmakers who would define the Glashutte aesthetic: architectural movements, three-quarter plates, hand-engraved balance cocks, and a philosophy that finishing was not optional — it was the point.

After World War II, the Soviet government nationalised the East German watch industry. The company lay dormant for 45 years — until 1994, when Walter Lange and Gunter Blumlein resurrected it. On 24 October 1994, four watches debuted simultaneously. The watch world has never been the same.

In a world of silicon hairsprings and laser-adjusted balances, the L951.5 is a deliberate act of defiance — a declaration that the human hand still has something irreplaceable to contribute.

— WatchTime Magazine, on the L951.5 calibre

Care and Service

This is a hand-wound mechanical watch. Wind the crown gently — 15 to 20 turns every two to three days — until you feel resistance. Do not force it past the point of resistance.

The chronograph pushers are not water-sealed the same way the case is — avoid pressing them when submerged. The alligator leather strap should be kept away from prolonged moisture.

Service interval: every 8 to 10 years. When serviced by a qualified watchmaker, the movement should outlast several lifetimes.
