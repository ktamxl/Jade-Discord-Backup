The Complication

The Watch That Knows the Calendar

A watch with a perpetual calendar knows that February has 28 days — and in a leap year, 29. It adjusts automatically through the entire Gregorian cycle, requiring correction only once every 400 years. An annual calendar, more modestly, knows the difference between 30 and 31 days for all months except February.

The annual calendar was an invention of Patek Philippe in 1996 — but it was A. Lange and Sohne who arguably perfected it. The L051.3 calibre integrates the annual calendar mechanism with the restrained, classical aesthetic of the 1815 family: Arabic numerals, a subsidiary seconds dial, and a moon phase display that achieves accuracy to one day in 122.6 years.

The moon phase is not merely decorative. In the era before atomic timekeeping, the 29.5-day lunar cycle governed tides, agricultural calendars, and religious observances across every civilisation. To carry that cycle mechanically — in a device no larger than a thumbnail — is one of watchmaking most most poetic achievements.

The Dial

Argente — Silver Quietest Voice

The argente dial — literally silver-coloured — is not sterling silver. It is a proprietary alloy developed to resist oxidation. Over decades, a well-maintained argente dial develops a subtle, even patina that gives it a warmth no modern lacquered dial can replicate.

The Arabic numerals on the 1815 Annual Calendar are not printed. They are cast in 18-karat gold, hand-polished on a cork wheel, and applied individually. Each one takes approximately 20 minutes to set. A single watchmaker spends an entire day setting the dial for one watch.

This is the economy of craft: it takes an enormous amount of time to make something that, at the end, looks effortless.

The annual calendar is the complication that rewards you every morning — because every morning it tells you the truth about the world without asking anything of you.

Care and Service

The L051.3 requires winding every two to three days to keep the calendar mechanism running. Unlike automatic watches that wind themselves from daily motion, this watch needs deliberate attention. Consider it a ritual: every second morning, wind it for 15 turns before you put it on.

Do not use the corrector pushers between 8pm and 4am — the date-change gear train is in motion. The correct procedure is to set the date by advancing the hands forward through midnight until the correct date appears.

When the watch is not being worn, lay it dial-up on a watch cushion. The alligator leather strap will last longer if it is unclasped and laid flat.
