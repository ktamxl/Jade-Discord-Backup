# Mr. Tam Ngong Chor — Eulogy (English Version)
**By Ken Tam**
**Funeral: 2026-03-30, Woodlawn Memorial Park, Colma, CA**

---

Dear family and friends,

We are gathered here today not only to mourn, but to celebrate. We are here to honor a man who built something out of nothing — a man whose legacy is not carved into stone, but written into the lives of the twenty family members gathered in this very room. We are here to remember our father — Mr. Tam Ngong Chor.

Dad was born on July 14, 1936, in Kaiping, Guangdong, China. His journey began in China, but his story is one of courage, of movement, and of hope that never gave out. In the 1950s, fleeing the upheaval of war, he left his hometown with his family and made his way to Hong Kong. It was there — through a chance introduction — that he met the love of his life, the woman who would be by his side for sixty years: our mother, Fong Lau Yin. They married in 1961, and over the next four years, the three of us were born. He built his family and grew his livelihood in Hong Kong, and even in the busiest of times, he always made sure to take us on trips — out into the Hong Kong countryside, exploring, adventuring together.

Then in 1972, for the sake of his growing family's future, Dad and Mom made a courageous decision: to leave behind everything they had built in Hong Kong and start over in America. They arrived in San Francisco with three young children and very little else.

We all know this part of the story. Those early years in America were genuinely hard. There was the language barrier, the financial pressure, and the loneliness of being far from home. His first job was at Sun Hung Heung Restaurant in Chinatown — and he stayed there, working faithfully, until the day he retired.

The work was demanding, but Dad never wavered in one thing: keeping the family together. On his days off, he insisted that everyone come home for dinner — and he was always the one at the stove. That was his way of saying *I love you*. Gathering around a table. Cooking for the people he loved.

In his later years, he had time to enjoy life — watching the horses at the racetrack, the occasional trip to the casino with friends, and above all, helping to raise his grandchildren. He lived his final chapter surrounded by family, and by every measure, he was a happy man.

The family he built has grown from three children into a family of twenty. He leaves behind two sons, five grandchildren, and four great-grandchildren. What started as one man's leap of faith across an ocean has become a flourishing family — rooted now, permanent, ours.

Dad passed peacefully on March 8th, at the age of ninety, with family by his side. He lived a full and complete life. Though his departure leaves an ache in our hearts, there is comfort in the natural order of things: when one generation moves on, a new generation rises to take their place. The newest members of our family are proof that his story goes on. His face may no longer be with us, but his spirit — his strength, his warmth, his stubborn love for this family — lives on in every one of us.

Dad, there are two things I want to say before we let you go.

The first is *goodbye* — and we mean it with everything we have.

The second is *thank you*. Thank you for working so hard to raise us and to teach us. Thank you for those weekly family dinners that you never let anyone skip. Thank you for crossing an ocean for us. Thank you for your sacrifice, and thank you for your love.

Dad — rest now. You've more than earned it.
