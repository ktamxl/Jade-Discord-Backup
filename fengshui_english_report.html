The ancients long ago found their answer to 子午沖: the wisest solution is to bury the husband and wife in adjacent but separate plots, allowing each energy field its own completeness, free from interference — yet at the larger scale of the landscape, they remain in each other's radiance.

Once the plots were separated, another question arose: would sharing a single headstone rekindle the energy mingling and revive the clash of 子 and 午?

"The way of heaven and earth is nothing other than the two breaths of yin and yang. When yin and yang each settle into their rightful place, ten thousand things find harmony naturally. CHAPTER FOUR

Lessons from Emperors — On the Limits of Feng Shui

"There is no such thing as a free lunch."
— An eternal truth, and feng shui is no exception.

In Chinese history, the art of feng shui was once treated as a state secret. The imperial court maintained the 欽天監 — the Bureau of Astronomy and Geography — whose officers devoted themselves entirely to the study of 堪輿, ensuring that the feng shui arrangements of the imperial tombs were beyond reproach. And yet history, turning through its great cycles of rise and fall, tells us again and again: feng shui is a tool, not a miracle. It can help a person weather a low tide; it cannot arrest the corruption of a human heart. It can seize the right moment; it cannot replace wisdom and virtue.

The Changling of Emperor Gaozu — Four Hundred Years of Han, and What Feng Shui Could and Could Not Do

The founder of the Han dynasty, Emperor Gaozu Liu Bang, rests at Changling on the Xianyang plateau, a site backed by the Jiujun Mountains, fronted by the Wei River, with the Jing River sweeping in from the left as the Azure Dragon and the Qi River flanking from the right as the White Tiger — all four guardian presences assembled in perfect formation. To find this plot, the great strategist Zhang Liang and the feng shui masters of early Han spent years traveling the length of the Guanzhong plain, until they discovered here what they recognized as a true "imperial穴位" — mountain and water embracing in a full circle of 氣, shaped like a lotus cradling the sun.

The Han Empire endured for four hundred years — one of the longest continuous dynasties in Chinese history. Yet even this most celebrated of feng shui configurations could not prevent Wang Mang's usurpation, could not shore up the weakened Eastern Han, could not stop the Yellow Turban uprising, the fracturing of the Three Kingdoms, the final dissolution of empire. The feng shui of Changling gave the Han a magnificent beginning. It could not stand in for the wisdom of each generation of emperors who came after.

Feng shui can assist fortune; it cannot manufacture destiny. That is the first lesson Changling offers those who come later.

The Zhaoling of Emperor Taizong — The Great Khan's Choice and Its Shadows

Tang Taizong, Li Shimin, was perhaps the greatest emperor in Chinese history. His mausoleum, the Zhaoling, was hewn into the summit of Jiuzong Mountain — the mountain itself becoming the tomb, inaugurating an entirely new style of Tang imperial burial. Jiuzong Mountain is immense and muscular, its ridges rising like a dragon ascending through cloud; Zhaoling holds its peak, gazing down over the Guanzhong plain, with eight hundred li of the Wei River valley laid out in unbroken view below.

And yet in his later years, Tang Taizong fell into another trap that feng shui cannot prevent: he consumed extraordinary national resources to build this monument to his own eternity, while neglecting to cultivate the successors who would carry the dynasty forward. The feng shui of Zhaoling sustained the Tang's great age, but it could not prevent the rise of Wu Zetian, could not hold back the catastrophe of the An-Shi Rebellion. After its height of glory, the empire began its long and irreversible descent.

Every gain entails a corresponding loss — that is Zhaoling's lesson for those who come after. However supreme the feng shui, if the balance of governance is lost, the mountains and rivers remain while the dynasty grows old.

The Xiaoling of Zhu Yuanzhang — Two Hundred and Seventy-Six Years of Ming

The tomb of the Ming dynasty's founder, Emperor Taizu Zhu Yuanzhang, rests on the southern face of Purple Mountain (Zijin Shan) outside Nanjing. Purple Mountain, known in antiquity as Bell Mountain, is shaped like a coiled dragon; the Xiaoling sits at the dragon's southern base, backed by the dragon's head, facing the open plain ahead, with flanking ridges embracing it like a jade sash. The 欽天監 devoted more than twenty years to choosing this site, calling on hundreds of thousands of laborers — the result was a burial complex that later generations would call "the crown of Ming and Qing imperial tombs."

The feng shui of Xiaoling did indeed shelter the Great Ming for two hundred and seventy-six years. Yet Zhu Yuanzhang himself, in his last years, massacred the very founding ministers of his dynasty — believing this would clear the way for the Zhu family's eternal dominance. Instead, he had eliminated the capable and the loyal, leaving a dynasty with no one left to sustain it. Two centuries on, the last Ming emperor, Chongzhen, ended the dynasty in a moment of solitary grief. It was not bad feng shui that brought the Ming down — it was institutional rot and a people's shattered trust.

Feng shui can protect the mountains and rivers. It cannot protect the human heart. That is Xiaoling's heaviest lesson.

Feng Shui's True Place — A Tool, Not a God

Three emperors, three stories, one conclusion: feng shui is among the most exquisite tools the natural world offers us. It helps human beings achieve the best possible resonance with the 氣 field of their environment, making good fortune more likely to arrive and difficulty easier to pass through. But it is finally just a tool — like a fine blade in a cook's hands. The better the blade, the more the cook's skill can be expressed; but however fine the blade, if the cook is careless, no great meal will emerge.

For the Tam family, this land of 坐丁向癸 is that finest blade. It can give support in the low valleys of a descendant's life; it can help the family recognize and seize what opportunity brings; it can provide the invisible emotional bond when the family needs its coherence most. But it cannot replace Ken's devoted heart, cannot substitute for the daily choices each descendant makes — to live carefully, to treat others with kindness, to build a life of quiet worth.

There is no such thing as a free lunch. Good feng shui is a gift — but a gift that requires effort, wisdom, and virtue to fully unfold. One part feng shui, seven parts human endeavor, two parts timing, one part heaven's will — so the ancients said, and so it remains true today.

CHAPTER FIVE

The Philosophy of the I Ching — Between Life, Death, and the Flow of Yin and Yang

The I Ching says: "The alternation of yin and yang is called the Way." Life and death are the greatest yin and yang in all the universe. The living dwell in yang; the departed dwell in yin. And yet within yin there is yang, and within yang there is yin — death is not an ending but a transformation of energy, as water becomes cloud, cloud becomes rain, and rain returns again to earth.

既濟卦 — The Philosophy of Completion

坐丁向癸 corresponds to 既濟卦 (the hexagram of Completion, ䷾) in the I Ching — water above (坎), fire below (離), water and fire in mutual fulfillment, each in its proper place. Among the sixty-four hexagrams, 既濟 is the only one in which all six lines occupy their correct positions. Its name means "the crossing is complete" — it is the symbol of perfect fulfillment.

And yet the I Ching's deeper teaching is this: after 既濟 comes 未濟卦 (the hexagram of Before Completion, ䷿) — fire above, water below, the two reversed, returning to a new beginning. After completion comes a new cycle; after ending comes a new start. The departure of an ancestor is one life's 既濟; the growth of descendants is the next generation's 未濟. Generation following generation, breath following breath — this is the very essence of the Way.

How the Living and the Ancestors Respond to One Another

The 彖辭 of the I Ching's 坤卦 (Earth hexagram) says: "Profound is the primal power of Earth — all things draw from it their being." The earth is the mother of all living things, and the storehouse of life's energy. When an ancestor is laid to rest in the earth, their spiritual field does not disperse — it merges with the 地氣 and continues, in a broader and more encompassing way, to touch the lives of those who carry the same blood.

This influence is not superstition. It is a form of resonance. Chinese medicine calls it "like energies seeking one another"; physics calls it "resonant frequency"; feng shui calls it 氣場感應. Different languages, the same underlying truth. Descendants and ancestors share a blood line, carry similar genetic codes, and at the invisible level form a natural energetic bond.

Yet this resonance is not automatic. It requires the "wholehearted attention" of the living to bring it alive. Wholehearted attention means: returning regularly to offer reverence, speaking to the ancestors with sincerity; coming to sit quietly on this land at the significant moments of life, feeling the movement of 地氣; passing the stories of those who came before to the generation that comes after, so that memory becomes the bridge across which the current flows.

The deeper the attention, the more alive the response. This is not mysticism. This is the physics of love.

The Balance of Destiny Within 坐丁向癸

In the I Ching, 丁 fire corresponds to the soft line of 離 hexagram — the fire of civilization, the light of wisdom. 癸 water corresponds to the core line of 坎 hexagram — the strength of yang at the heart of the deep. 丁 fire at the back (the sitting position) means the ancestor illuminates with the light of wisdom; 癸 water at the face (the facing position) means descendants flow forward into the wider world with the adaptability of water.

This is the ideal form of protection: the ancestor shines from behind; the descendant flows forward. Luminance does not block the current; the current does not extinguish the light. Each fulfills the other — together composing the truest image of what it means for the mountains and rivers to hold memory and care.

We have also reserved the plot beside him for your grandmother — so that in another realm, they continue side by side. 子 and 午, left and right, clashing without wounding, separated without severed. This is the last thing we of this generation could do for them. It was done with all the care we had.

But what I want to tell you is this: feng shui is what we can give — yet it is not what matters most. What matters most is whether you remember his name. Whether you know something of what he lived through. Whether, on some Sunday evening at the dinner table, you let the story of him fall naturally into the conversation, a sentence or two, for your children to carry forward.

There is no such thing as a free lunch. This is not a warning — it is an invitation. An invitation to take your life seriously; to understand that behind every beautiful thing, someone once gave quietly and without fanfare. The good feng shui of that land is heaven's generosity; but how much it can offer you depends on how much sincerity you bring when you come, and how carefully you live your days.

Good feng shui is like a good teacher. A good teacher can show you the road. But a teacher cannot walk your road for you.

I hope you will return often. Not for any other reason — only to sit for a while among the green grass and the quiet wind, and feel what it is to know: before you, someone prepared the road; after you, someone will walk on what you have prepared. That is what family means. That is feng shui's deepest secret. And it is the most ordinary and the most luminous poem that life ever writes.

With care and reverence,
Sisi

‧ 榮 (Flourishing) — The generation character of 譚國偉 (Ken Tam), who carries the line forward, honoring the family name with his flourishing
